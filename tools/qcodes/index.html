Operational notes
Q-codes originated in commercial Morse traffic at the start of the twentieth century and are still used universally on CW and SSB — they collapse a whole question or statement into three characters, which matters when conditions are bad. The same code reads as a question with a rising inflection (or a trailing "?") and as a statement otherwise — "QTH?" versus "QTH Hermosa Beach". Prosigns are sent as a single concatenated Morse character with no inter-letter space; written here as two overlined letters (AR, SK, BT) they are heard as one sound. Prosigns frame a transmission — CT to open, AR to end a message, SK to clear — and replace the punctuation a teletype operator would otherwise have to send. NATO phonetic alphabet (ICAO / ITU spelling alphabet) is the modern international standard for voice. The digits use "tree", "fower", "fife", "niner" precisely to avoid being confused with "three", "four", "five", "nine" under noise. Western Union spelling is the older US convention you sometimes hear on CW nets and from old-timer phone operators — geographic place names (Boston, Chicago, Denver, New York) plus common first names. Not standard internationally, but commonly understood domestically.
